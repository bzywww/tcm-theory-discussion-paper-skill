# Evidence Rules

## Classical Evidence

Use classical sources functionally. Each quotation should support one clear role:

- **Concept definition**: what the core term means.
- **Physiological basis**: normal relation among organs, qi, blood, fluids, spirit, meridians.
- **Etiology**: how disease begins.
- **Pathogenesis**: how the mechanism forms.
- **Transmission**: how one link leads to another.
- **Treatment principle**: why the method follows.
- **Formula logic**: why this formula matches this pattern.

Do not use one formula record to prove a broad universal disease law. Do not claim the ancients already proposed a modern theory; instead, show conceptual compatibility or pathogenesis correspondence.

## Modern Mechanism

Use modern mechanisms as a bridge, not the main body.

Good uses:

- explain why a clinical phenomenon is plausible;
- clarify one difficult pathogenesis link;
- offer measurable variables for future study;
- support a treatment or rehabilitation scenario.

Bad uses:

- long pathway lists;
- brain-region or molecular correspondences to TCM concepts without need;
- modern theory as the article's only real structure;
- TCM concepts pasted after a biomedical review.

Preferred relation:

**TCM as root, modern mechanism as clarification. Classical logic leads; modern evidence sharpens a specific link.**

## Literature Role Assignment

Assign each source to a role:

- target-journal literature: style, avoidance, local academic conversation;
- classical literature: theoretical root;
- modern clinical literature: disease carrier and problem reality;
- modern mechanism literature: mechanism bridge;
- formula literature: treatment and prescription logic.

Never pretend title/abstract-only sources are full-text evidence.
